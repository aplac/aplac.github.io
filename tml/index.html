Project Description

Thakbong, Defining the Undefinable

The Thakbong project is an open-ended project, which in its current stage can best be described as a digital endeavor to document, archive and research epigraphy in the Taiwan Strait and beyond. The documentation consist of primary data, mostly photos, and secondary data which are derived through annotation. Annotation involves the transcription of inscriptions, as well as the classification and description of sites, objects, physical or semiotic and events. Classification refers to a closed set of labels, useful for analysis techniques as used in the Digital Humanities, while descriptions target mainly people living in other times or other regions, who cannot or can no longer visit the documented sites. The descriptions thus try to give them the best possible impression of sites and objects which unfortunately are mostly bound to disappear.

The Orientation of Houses

Responsibe: Oliver Streiter
This project can be understood as a sub-project of either the the Thakbong sub-project "Geologies, Geographies and Cultures in Southeast Asia" or the Thakbong sub-project "Documenting Culture and Resilience in Southeast Asia".
This project simply tries to capture the orientation of houses in relation to their georeferences, so that this orientation can be related to environmental condiitions, such as the slope and its direction, the location of the sea or the main wind direction.
Most data in this project have been collected in Summer 2021 when Oliver Streiter spent several months in Penghu.

Temple Bells in Pacific Asia

Responsibe: Oliver Streiter
Bells, like temple bells, if inscribed, are epigraphic objects of a particular kind.
As a sound instrument, they contribute to the soundscape of a temple, and overlay the soundscape of a community partially with that of the temple. The soundscape of bells and temples thus belong to the intangible heritage of a community and merit to be preserved and researched.
Secondly, bells, implicitly or explicitly, have also been used to structure time. This is e.g. very visible in the way that time is indicated in Thai. The way when the bells are struck and when not is an important way to understand how time is or has been structured in a community.
Third, the sound properties of the bell are formed by the physical properties of the bell. It's material, it's shape and the type of clappers.
Bells are also very interesting for the study of early settler history. As first generations of settlers wouldn't have the means to create bells locally, in the area of the new settlement, most bells would be acquired and the inscriptions on the bells, mentioning the foundry, allow to trace back economic relations, frequently with the region from where the settlers migrated from.
