s175: Wat Prok

Name: Wat Prok, Wat Prok Yannawa, วัปรกยานนาวา,
Type: Mon Wat, Mon spiritual site
ID: s175
Description:
A Mon temple in Sathon. According to the description provided by the Bangkok government, the temple has been built in 1927.
The east side of the temple borders with the Chaozhou graveyard. The north side borders with the temple -Bogong Gong- of the Chaozhou graveyard. To the south is the Wat Prok 1 Alley. Entrance and exit to the monastery are to the 30 Soi Wat Prok. This temple should not be confounded with the Wat Nak Prok at the Thomburi side.
The temple Wat Prok contains a Buddha image of white jade and a main chedi in Sri Lankan style. The outer wall and the gate of the temple show statues and images of the Hamsa, the Mon national symbol, known in Burmese as Hintha and in Thai as Hong.
The overall style of the temple is called Hongsawadih style, where Hongsawadih refers to the ancient capital of Pegu. Pegu was the capital (1740-1757) of the Mon Restored Hanthawaddy Kingdom, before its annexation by Burma. Pegu is known in modern-days as Bago.
According to some web-pages, the monastery offers courses in Mon language. Also, rituals are still held in Mon language. These facts need to be verified.
On the east and west side of the wat, the inner walls contain niches for funerary urns. Most niches are empty. On the east side the niches contain the ashes of some recently deceased monks, presumably of this monastery. All slates covering the niches have been documented in 2024 by Oliver.
The temple and its location might be considered in relation to the other spiritial, religious or burial sites nearby which are not cental to Thai culture. The Vishnu-Temple in front to the Wat Prok Yannawa, the Chaozhou graveyard nearby, the Java-Mosque and its graveyard just behind the Chaozhou graveyard, further to the north the Catholic area of St. Louis, with hospital, school, church and ambassy, then even further to the north, the Chinese-Christian, Parsi, Hakka, Hokkien, Hainan, and Cantonese cemeteries, there is a long line of foreign encroachments, which very likely relate to the history and geography of the used spaces.
Hypotheses:
We hypothesize, that at the time of the erection of these sites, around 1900, the foreign cemeteries, temples and churches were outside the city of Bangkok.
We hypothesize, that the area of the foreign cemeteries, temples and churches seemed of little use to the Thai people. Probably, because this area was easily and frequently flooded.
